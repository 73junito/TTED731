## Introduction

Adult learners in postsecondary Automotive Technology programs need instruction that is practical, flexible, and directly connected to workplace expectations. Many students are balancing school with employment, family responsibilities, military transition, or career change. Because of these responsibilities, instructional design must do more than deliver technical information. It must help students build confidence, apply knowledge, and develop professional diagnostic reasoning.

This instructional design and delivery plan focuses on automotive electrical diagnostics. Electrical diagnosis is often challenging because students must interpret wiring diagrams, use service information, operate diagnostic equipment, and make decisions based on evidence. The plan integrates adult learning principles, Universal Design for Learning, self-regulated learning, and technology-supported instruction through the Car Diagnosis Simulator: https://car-diagnosis-simulator.vercel.app/

## Learner Population

The target population is adult learners enrolled in a postsecondary Automotive Technology program at a technical or community college. This population may include recent high school graduates, veterans, working adults, career changers, and experienced technicians returning for updated skills. Students bring different levels of prior knowledge. Some are confident in hands-on tasks but need support with theory, reading, documentation, or electrical systems. Others may understand classroom content but need more guided practice in the laboratory.

This instructional plan recognizes those differences by combining direct instruction, hands-on practice, simulation, reflection, and flexible learning options.

## Learning Objectives

At the end of the module, students will be able to:

1. Interpret automotive wiring diagrams and manufacturer service information.
2. Apply a systematic diagnostic process to electrical faults.
3. Safely use digital multimeters, scan tools, and related diagnostic equipment.
4. Analyze test results to determine probable root causes.
5. Document diagnostic reasoning and recommend evidence-based repairs.
6. Reflect on diagnostic decisions to improve future performance.

These objectives move beyond memorization and require students to apply, analyze, evaluate, and communicate technical information.

## Delivery Methods

### Face-to-Face Delivery

Face-to-face instruction will include instructor demonstrations, shop safety review, guided laboratory practice, and team-based troubleshooting. Students will observe diagnostic procedures before practicing them on training vehicles or lab boards. Immediate feedback from the instructor will help students correct mistakes and build confidence.

### Hybrid Delivery

Hybrid delivery will combine online preparation with hands-on laboratory instruction. Before class, students will review videos, readings, wiring diagrams, and simulator scenarios. Classroom time will then focus on application, discussion, and live diagnostics.

### Synchronous Delivery

Synchronous delivery will include live class discussions, demonstrations, question-and-answer sessions, and virtual office hours. Students can discuss diagnostic strategies in real time and receive clarification from the instructor.

### Asynchronous Delivery

Asynchronous delivery will support adult learners who need schedule flexibility. Students will complete recorded lessons, discussion posts, reflection journals, quizzes, and Car Diagnosis Simulator activities outside scheduled class time.

## Instructional Strategies

The instructional framework uses problem-based learning, scenario-based learning, cognitive apprenticeship, guided practice, and reflection.

Problem-based learning begins with authentic customer complaints rather than isolated facts. For example, students may investigate a no-start condition, an intermittent lighting fault, or a battery drain concern. They must collect evidence, form hypotheses, perform tests, and justify their repair decisions.

Scenario-based learning helps students connect classroom instruction to workplace practice. Each scenario includes customer concerns, vehicle history, diagnostic trouble codes, wiring diagrams, and test results.

Cognitive apprenticeship is used when the instructor models expert thinking. The instructor explains why a test is selected, what result is expected, and how the result affects the next diagnostic step. Support is gradually reduced as students gain skill and independence.

Reflection is included after each diagnostic activity. Students explain what they tested, why they tested it, what they learned, and what they would do differently next time.

## Technology Integration

Technology is central to this instructional design. Students will use the Car Diagnosis Simulator as an AI-supported instructional environment for practicing diagnostic reasoning before and after live lab work.

Live application: https://car-diagnosis-simulator.vercel.app/  
GitHub repository: https://github.com/73junito/car-diagnosis-simulator

The simulator gives students a low-risk environment to practice systematic troubleshooting. Students can review symptoms, analyze diagnostic data, make decisions, and receive formative feedback. This supports adult learners because they can repeat scenarios, learn at their own pace, and prepare for hands-on laboratory activities.

The simulator does not replace live vehicle work. Instead, it prepares students for it. Students use simulation to build confidence, then apply those skills in the shop.

## Universal Design for Learning

Universal Design for Learning is included to support diverse adult learners.

### Multiple Means of Engagement

Students engage through authentic automotive problems, simulator challenges, team diagnostics, and career-relevant scenarios. Students are more likely to persist when they understand why the activity matters.

### Multiple Means of Representation

Content is presented through demonstrations, videos, diagrams, written procedures, service information, simulator activities, and hands-on examples.

### Multiple Means of Action and Expression

Students demonstrate learning through lab performance, written diagnostic reports, oral explanations, simulator decisions, reflection journals, and peer discussion.

## Student Engagement Methods

Student engagement will be supported through active learning and workplace relevance. Activities include:

- Weekly diagnostic challenges
- Team troubleshooting
- Simulator-based case studies
- ASE-style practice questions
- Peer coaching
- Reflection journals
- Exit tickets
- Instructor feedback conferences

These methods encourage students to participate actively rather than passively receive information.

## Conclusion

This instructional design plan provides a flexible, authentic, and technology-enhanced approach to teaching automotive electrical diagnostics to adult learners. By combining face-to-face, hybrid, synchronous, and asynchronous delivery methods, the plan addresses the needs of students with different schedules, experiences, and confidence levels.

The Car Diagnosis Simulator strengthens the design by giving students repeated opportunities to practice diagnostic reasoning in a low-risk environment. When combined with hands-on laboratory instruction, reflection, UDL, and problem-based learning, the simulator supports workforce readiness and lifelong learning in Automotive Technology education.
